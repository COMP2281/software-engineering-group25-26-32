Updated:
This system and its intended uses are on the surface are socially and ethically positive as it provides accessibility to users as they can search for theses semantically rather than exact word match. This makes academic material easier to discover for students, researchers and the regular public who may not always aligned terminology wise with a theses they are trying to search for. The system practically reduces one of the common barriers in academic repositories, where relevant work is often missed simply because search terms do not match the stored title precisely.The summarisation feature strengthen the accessibility as it allows users to judge relevance and material without going through long theses.

A key positive impact this system has potential for is to increase the reach for a lot of academic papers that otherwise remain unused and unreferenced. The Ethos collection contains valuable research which is harder explore due to traditional search tools .By integrating sematic retrieval, the platform increases visibility of research across disciplines and helps users discover related work even when terminology differs between subject areas. This can support broader academic engagement and mayy especially help interdisciplinary users who search across topics where naming conventions are inconsistent.

The benefits of the system can keep branching off but we must recognise that there is potential for ethical and societal concerns that can be linked to our search tool whether in its current or future state. The semantic model relies on external and pre-trained language representation and vector indexing methods. Since the embedding model was trained before being applied to this thesis domain, its retrieval behaviour may reflect biases present in the original training data. In practice, this means certain writing styles, common academic phrasing or dominant terminology may be favoured over less common language patterns. Theses using older terminology, highly specialised wording or unconventional title structures may therefore rank lower even when they are highly relevant.

The AI summarisation feature poses another ethical concern as by summarising a theses it is essentially reducing a long academic paper into a much shorter representation. While this improves efficiency and usability, it may also reduce nuance or remove important context. Methodological limitations, careful wording, or cautious conclusions may be simplified too heavily when converted into a short summary. In some cases, this could lead users to misunderstand the emphasis or significance of the original research if they rely on the summary without reading the full thesis.

OCR processing too creates a concern here as the quality of retrieval depends on the accuracy of digitisation of handwritten these which only exist in scanned form. If the handwriting is unrecognisable then the extracted text may contain errors that weaken search accuracy or summarisation quality. This means some research may become less visible not because it is less relevant, but because its source format produces weaker machine readable text. This brings up unfairness is the thesis archives as the model unintentionally favours typed theses. These technicalities punish old theses regardless of their academic quality and material.

In future development, these impacts may become stronger if the system expands to larger repositories or adds more automated decision making features. Full text indexing, recommendation systems or better summarization would make things easier but also increase the system’s control over which research is prioritized and how it is understood. For this reason, future development should keep improving access while ensuring transparency, so users understand that the system aids academic discovery without replacing critical academic judgment.

## Project Problem Statement: Surfacing knowledge from doctoral research - mining the hidden potential of international doctoral theses
This document gave us a brief, surface-level idea of the project. It provided the context of the dataset as well as a short summary of the client's progress with respect to previous prototypes. It also mentions the desperate need for a search tool which facilitates efficient and effective searches instead of having to laboriously analyse theses by-hand. The problem statement did not just introduce the project context; it directly shaped our decision to prioritise semantic retrieval over traditional keyword search, since the core problem identified was the inefficiency of manually locating relevant theses within a very large repository.

## Journal Article: Exploring rurality and ethnicity in globalised higher education: ideologies, intersections and narratives in doctoral research theses
This journal article written by our client argues that rurality is politicised in higher education systems and the higher education institutions frequently reproduce marginalisation of rural and ethnic communities through ideology, deficit narratives, and institutional practices. Overall, the article helps to highlight our client's intention in 'decolonising' research in international education. 

## Journal Article: Surfacing “Southern” Perspectives on Student Engagement With Internationalization: Doctoral Theses as Alternative Forms of Knowledge - Catherine Montgomery
This journal article also written by our client claims that the research in the field of international education produced by international students from the 'Global South' (the areas of knowledge that have been marginalised by dominant Western academic canons) often reproduces Western theory and methodological frameworks.

## Research Article: Unveiling crisis in globalised higher education: Artificial intelligence insights from doctoral research in EThOS - Catherine Montgomery, Craig Stewart, Olanrewaju Aduragba, Francesca Poli
This research article discusses to what extent AI can be used and how effective it is in analysing large portions of research for the sake of viewing 'crises' in higher education from a perspective alternative to the global social, political, and technological tensions which often cause HE to seem to be in crisis. The article concludes that AI can analyse large datasets very quickly, is able to identify broad patterns, and can discover themes efficiently, but stresses that it should complement, not replace human analysis. This is because AI misses deeper insights gathered by humans like intersectional issues, contextual analysis of crises, and links between economic crisis and political discourse. The research article influenced our design philosophy by suggesting that AI should support academic interpretation rather than replace it; this is reflected in our decision to keep the full thesis accessible and to return page numbers alongside AI-generated content.

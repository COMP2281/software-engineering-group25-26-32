# Concern 1: Users may not have a technical background, so the system must be simple to learn and use

The user demographic is researchers, where many most likely won't have much of a technical background, which is why the system was designed with a minimialist UI and simple usability such as the search bar, clearly labelled controls, and a straightfroward results page. 

The elementary UI design helps to remove the learning-curve barrier in understanding how to use the tool effectively. Core actions such as entering a query, applying filters, viewing a thesis, generating a summary, and asking a question about a thesis are all exposed through obvious interface elements rather than requiring specialist commands, knowledge, or relative experience in navigating the UI. In the words of our client, we believe that we've successfully designed the tool with simplicity: 

*"The front end and design of the tool is actually very good. I like the simplicity of it and the way each thesis has a drop-down arrow which can be expanded or collapsed while still maintaining the full search list. The functions for each individual thesis are very well set out and easy to navigate. I like the way the individual thesis page indicates the date and whether it has an abstract."*

# Concern 2: Searching thousands of theses manually is slow and overwhelming

A key usability issue in the project context is that researchers would otherwise need to inspect large numbers of theses by hand. This creates high cognitive load and makes the research process inefficient.

The semantic similarity feature addresses this concern. By returning a ranked list of the theses (via title & abstract) that closely matched the semantic meaning of the user's search query, the burden of finding the most relevant theses by-hand is significantly reduced. Additional user-friendly features such as the author, year, and subject discipline filters further narrow down a user's search for relevant theses. 

It must be mentioned that the semantic similarity feature is open to optimisation as each thesis' semantic embedding is computed from that thesis' title and abstract, whereas an embedding computed from that thesis' actual content would most likely provide a more accurate embedding and hence contribute to an improved set of results for the user. This issue has been discussed with the client as a possible future development as it has not yet been possible to implement given the limited available resources of the project (the token limit of the Gemini API specifically). Regardless, the following comments from our client demonstrates the significant progress made in from the existing model: 

*"This is a fantastic tool that offers huge potential in exploring and summarising doctoral theses. I am really excited about it and I think it has definite potential to inform our further research work in this area."*

*"I also really like the ‘ask a question about the thesis’ function. That is particularly great! Very good idea. It helps to focus the exploration of the thesis for particular purposes."*

# Concern 3: Users need to judge relevance quickly without opening every thesis

Despite the fact that the returned theses already reduces the level of exploration required in finding relevant theses, there is still the high cognitive load of exploring each thesis by-hand to determine whether it's of particular benefit to the user. 

The thesis metadata (such as the year and author) and particularly each thesis' relevance score enables the user to scan the list of results and make rapid judgements on theses without necessarily missing theses that would be key to a user's research. 

# Concern 4: AI-generated outputs must be useful and trustworthy

Although the tool facilitates efficient thesis discovery, AI isn't completely trustworthy and could potentially mislead researchers - a major flaw if ignored. 

This is exactly why we made sure to make the inclusion of page numbers when gathering information from a thesis an absolute necessity. By explicitly requesting Gemini to include page numbers in its response, users are able to verify the authenticity of aspects of Gemini's response to their questions and its summaries of the theses. Additionally, Gemini's detailed response structure improves trust and interpretability.
